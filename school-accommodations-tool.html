Jump to what you need right now

Before your meeting, gather anything the school has already written about your child. Their own words are your strongest evidence — schools find it hard to argue with their own records.

You don't need everything before you start. But the more of the school's own documentation you can bring to a meeting, the stronger your position. Schools find it much harder to dismiss concerns when the evidence is their own words.

Report cards and progress reports. The teacher comment sections are often the most useful part — look for language about attention, completion, behavior, or social interaction. Save or print the full document, not just the grades.

Teacher emails and conference notes. Any written communication from a teacher about your child's performance or behavior is documentation. Search your email for the teacher's name or your child's school address. Print or save anything relevant.

Prior evaluation reports. If your child has ever been tested by the school (psychologist, speech-language pathologist, occupational therapist), those reports contain formal observations and scores. You are entitled to copies of all of them.

Behavior documentation. If your child has ever been sent home, had a behavior incident report filed, or been placed in in-school suspension, those records exist and you can request them.

Outside evaluations and letters. A report from a private psychologist, pediatric neurologist, or therapist carries significant weight — especially when it includes specific accommodation recommendations. Ask your provider to write a letter if you don't have a formal report.

Your right to records. Under FERPA (the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act), you have the right to inspect and request copies of all educational records the school maintains on your child — at no cost to you. Send a written request to the principal or special education coordinator. The school must respond within 45 days. You do not need to give a reason.

Tip: When you request records, ask specifically for "all educational records, including any behavioral records, evaluation reports, progress monitoring data, and communications regarding my child." A general request sometimes produces incomplete results.

There are two types of school support plans. An IEP provides specialized teaching. A 504 provides accommodations. Most parents start here to figure out which one their child needs.

IEP (Individualized Education Program) — under IDEA, provides specialized instruction and related services. Requires formal eligibility in one of 13 disability categories. The IEP team (which includes you) develops measurable goals and specifies services. Reviewed annually; re-evaluated every three years.

504 Plan — under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act. Provides accommodations only — no specialized instruction, just a level playing field. Broader eligibility than IDEA: any physical or mental impairment that substantially limits a major life activity, including learning, concentrating, or communicating. Often faster to obtain than an IEP. Commonly used for ADHD, anxiety, sensory differences, and chronic illness.

When to pursue an IEP: Your child needs specialized teaching (not just accommodations), or related services like speech therapy or OT, and qualifies in one of IDEA's 13 categories.

When to pursue a 504: Your child has a documented disability that creates barriers, but doesn't need specialized instruction — they just need the conditions to access learning equally.

When to push from 504 to IEP: If your child has a 504 in place and is still not making meaningful progress, that's a signal to request a comprehensive evaluation under IDEA.

These are the most common things schools can put in a plan — grouped by what they help with. Use Build Your Case to get a list tailored to your child's specific diagnosis.

Time & Pacing: Extended time on tests and assignments, chunked assignments with interim deadlines, flexible scheduling for tests, reduced homework load.

Environment: Preferential seating, small group or separate testing room, noise-reducing headphones, access to a quiet workspace, reduced visual clutter near workspace.

Presentation & Format: Written instructions in addition to verbal, text-to-speech tools, audio versions of texts, visual schedules, graphic organizers, highlighted or color-coded materials.

Response Flexibility: Oral responses instead of written, speech-to-text, scribe support, reduced writing requirements, calculator or spelling tools on non-spelling assessments.

Regulation & Sensory: Movement breaks, access to fidget tools, sensory breaks, scheduled check-ins with a trusted adult, a calm-down space.

Behavioral & Social: Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP), advance notice of transitions, modified social expectations, positive reinforcement systems, lunch or recess support.

Accommodations for elementary-aged children often focus on environmental and sensory needs — predictable routines, small groups, transition warnings, and break systems — since young children can't self-advocate for these in the moment.

You have more rights than most parents realize. The three that matter most right now: your child is entitled to real support (not just minimal help), the school must say no in writing if they decline anything you request, and you are an equal member of the team — not a guest.

IDEA Eligibility Categories — Ask for these by name
Other Health Impairment (OHI) ADHD, chronic health conditions
Specific Learning Disability (SLD) Dyslexia, dysgraphia, dyscalculia
Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) Autism, Asperger's
Emotional Disturbance (ED) Anxiety, depression, mood disorders

If the school says a diagnosis doesn't qualify, ask them in writing which category applies — and why the one you named does not.

FAPE (Free Appropriate Public Education): Your child is entitled to special education and related services at no cost to you, designed to meet their unique needs. In Endrew F. v. Douglas County School District (2017), the U.S. Supreme Court ruled that FAPE requires more than minimal progress — the IEP must be "reasonably calculated to enable a child to make progress appropriate in light of the child's circumstances." Schools sometimes use vague language about "appropriate" progress. The legal standard is meaningful progress, not merely showing up.

LRE (Least Restrictive Environment): Your child must be educated alongside non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate.

IDEA Eligibility Categories — What to Ask For by Name: Schools sometimes tell families that a diagnosis doesn't qualify for an IEP. Know the correct category names. ADHD qualifies under Other Health Impairment (OHI) — ask for this by name if the team is hesitant. Dyslexia, dysgraphia, and dyscalculia qualify under Specific Learning Disability (SLD). Autism qualifies under Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD). These are IDEA's statutory categories. If a school says a condition doesn't qualify, ask them in writing which eligibility category they believe applies and why the one you named does not.

10-Day Suspension Rule and Manifestation Determination: If your child is suspended for more than 10 cumulative school days in a school year, IDEA requires the school to conduct a Manifestation Determination Review (MDR). The MDR team must determine whether the behavior is a direct result of the disability or a failure to implement the IEP. If it is, the school cannot simply suspend or expel the student — they must address the underlying need. This rule exists to prevent schools from pushing out students whose behavior is disability-related. Track suspension days. The 10-day clock is cumulative, not consecutive.

Child Find: Schools are legally obligated to identify children with disabilities. You don't have to wait for the school to approach you. You can request an evaluation at any time.

Consent: The school must obtain your written consent before evaluating your child and before implementing an IEP for the first time.

Prior Written Notice (PWN): If the school refuses any of your requests — to evaluate, to provide a service, to change the plan — they must explain that refusal in writing. Request this whenever you receive a verbal "no."

Due Process: You have the right to dispute school decisions through mediation, a state complaint, or a due process hearing. In Texas, you can also file an OCR complaint for 504-related issues at no cost.

ARD-specific (Texas): In Texas, the IEP team meeting is called an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting. The ARD committee makes eligibility, placement, and plan decisions. You are an equal member of this committee — not a guest.

Getting a plan in place follows a sequence. You don't have to know all 8 steps — just the next one. The most important step is Step 3: putting your request in writing.

1. Document your observations. Before any formal request, write down specific, dated examples — behaviors at home, at school, during transitions. "Struggles with attention" is weak; "unable to complete a 10-minute task without four redirections" is evidence.

2. Make an informal inquiry. A conversation with your child's teacher or counselor can surface quick informal supports and help you understand what the school already knows. Informal supports are not legally binding and can be withdrawn at any time.

3. Submit a written evaluation request. This is the most important step. Send it dated, keep a copy, request written confirmation. The legal clock starts here. In Texas, the school typically has 60 school days after receiving your consent to complete the evaluation.

4. Sign consent for evaluation. Review the Permission to Evaluate form carefully. You can request additional areas of testing if something appears to be missing.

5. Participate in the evaluation. Complete rating scales honestly. Share outside evaluation records. You have the right to provide input throughout the process.

6. Attend the eligibility meeting (ARD). You are a member of this team. You can bring a support person. You are not required to sign at the meeting — you may take documents home first.

7. Develop the IEP or 504 Plan. Be specific — vague language in the plan means vague implementation. Ask who is responsible for each accommodation, how it will be tracked, and what happens if it isn't working.

8. Monitor, review, and follow up. Set a 4–6 week check-in after any new plan begins. Follow up everything in writing. You can request a meeting at any time — you do not have to wait for the annual review.

Exact words you can use in the room. Read these before your meeting. Screenshot the ones that feel hardest to say — having them on your phone means you don't have to remember them under pressure.

If they press you to sign at the meeting
"I'd like to take the documents home and review them before I sign. I understand this may delay the start date, and I'm comfortable with that tradeoff."
You are never required to sign at the meeting. Ever. This phrase ends that pressure.
If they say no to anything you've asked for
"If you're declining that request, I'd like it documented in writing along with the reason. I understand that's called a Prior Written Notice, and I'm entitled to request it."
A verbal no disappears. A written no is the start of a paper trail — and schools know it.
When things are moving too fast
"Before we move forward, I want to make sure I understand what this will look like in practice — not just what the document says."
This slows the room down. You are allowed to slow the room down.

Requesting an initial evaluation (in writing): "I am formally requesting a comprehensive evaluation of my child, [name], under IDEA and/or Section 504. I have concerns about [describe]. Please provide written acknowledgment and a copy of our procedural safeguards."

If they try to handle it verbally: "I understand you'd like to talk — I'm happy to do that. But I'd like my written request to remain on file, since that's what starts the evaluation timeline. Can we confirm in writing that the request was received?"

Requesting a 504 when you have documentation: "My child has a documented diagnosis of [condition], which substantially limits [area]. I'm requesting a 504 team meeting to develop a plan. What is the process and timeline from here?"

At the ARD meeting — slowing it down: "Before we move forward, I want to make sure I understand what this will look like in practice — not just what the document says. Who is responsible for this accommodation, how often, and how will it be tracked?"

If pressed to sign at the meeting: "I'd like to take the documents home and review them before I sign. I understand this may delay the start date, and I'm comfortable with that tradeoff."

Speaking for your elementary child: "As [child's] parent, I have information about their functioning that this evaluation cannot capture. What I observe at home is part of the full picture and belongs in this record. [Child's name] [describe specific behaviors, patterns, and what helps]."

Navigating the ARD without a professional advocate: "I'm here as [child's] parent — I don't have a professional advocate with me today, but I prepared some questions in advance and I want to understand everything before I agree to anything. I may need things explained more than once, and I may ask to table items I'm not ready to decide on."

When accommodations aren't being followed: "According to [child's] plan, they're supposed to receive [accommodation]. I've observed this isn't consistently happening. I'd like to understand what's going on and make sure this is resolved — and I'd like my concern documented if it continues."

Requesting a 504 when school proposes informal supports: "I appreciate the team's approach. I'd like those supports in writing as part of a formal 504 plan, because informal supports aren't enforceable. If my child changes teachers, I have no recourse. I'm not being adversarial — I just need something that travels with my child."

After the meeting — follow-up email: "Thank you for meeting on [date]. My understanding is that the plan includes [list]. Please let me know if I've missed or misunderstood anything."

Key phrases that establish your position: "This is a formal written request" · "I'd like this documented" · "Can you send written confirmation?" · "I'm not signing today" · "What specifically will look different in the classroom?"

If the plan exists but isn't being followed — or the school is saying no to things your child needs — you have options. Start by naming clearly which problem you have: the plan isn't being followed, the plan is wrong, or the school is refusing something entirely. Each has a different fix.

Name the problem clearly first. There are three distinct issues: (1) implementation gap — plan is reasonable, but it's not being followed; (2) wrong plan — accommodations aren't addressing the right needs; (3) eligibility dispute — the school is declining to provide services you believe your child needs. Each has a different solution.

Document before you escalate. Write down specific dates and examples, every conversation you've had about the issue and who said what, every email and its response, and what your child reports. Send follow-up emails after verbal conversations to create a paper trail.

Escalation path: Teacher or service provider → 504 coordinator or special education case manager → principal or director of special education → formal written request for a team meeting → Prior Written Notice (PWN) if the school declines.

Formal options: State complaint (Texas Education Agency, no attorney required, TEA investigates within 60 days) · Due process hearing (more formal, typically requires an attorney) · OCR complaint (U.S. Dept. of Education, for 504 issues, free, no attorney required) · Mediation (free, voluntary, neutral third party).

Filing a complaint doesn't end your collaborative relationship with the school. Knowing your options — and being willing to name them — often changes the dynamic before you ever have to use them.
